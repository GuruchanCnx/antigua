Sacred Places, Living History

San Juan del Obispo

Founded in the 1530s, this village is home to the palace of Francisco Marroquín (1499–1563), Guatemala’s first bishop and a key figure in early colonial history. He founded the first university in Central America and translated religious texts into Kaqchikel. The village remains a quiet guardian of this layered past.

Antigua Guatemala

Founded in 1543, Antigua was the colonial capital of Central America for over 200 years. Destroyed by earthquakes in 1773, it was abandoned but never forgotten. Today, its ruins, baroque churches, and cobblestone streets form a UNESCO World Heritage Site—a living museum of Spanish, Mayan, and mestizo culture.

Santo Domingo Hotel

Built on the ruins of a 16th-century Dominican monastery, this luxury hotel preserves original arches, fountains, and cloisters. Archaeological excavations revealed Mayan artifacts beneath, revealing layers of history—from pre-Columbian settlement to Spanish conquest to modern elegance.

Iximche Mayan Ruins

Capital of the Kaqchikel Maya from 1470 until its fall in 1524, Iximche was a powerful city-state with four main plazas, temples, and ball courts. It briefly allied with Spanish conquistador Pedro de Alvarado before resisting colonization. Today, it remains a sacred site for Mayan ceremonies and a symbol of resilience.
